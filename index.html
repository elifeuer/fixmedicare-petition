The Reforms We Demand

12 Critical Failures That Must Be Fixed

These are not abstract policy debates. They are real gaps that affect the health, dignity, and financial security of tens of millions of seniors every single day.

01 - ENROLLMENT
Late Enrollment Penalties: Part B and D

Beneficiaries who miss their initial enrollment window face permanent premium surcharges: 10% per year for Part B and up to 1% per month for Part D. These penalties fall hardest on individuals who delayed enrollment due to employer coverage transitions, creating a lasting financial burden through no real fault of their own.

02 - HOME CARE
Home Care: Access and Quality

Medicare's home health benefit remains severely restricted in scope and hours. Many beneficiaries who could live independently with adequate in-home support are instead forced into costly institutional care. Expanding coverage to include personal care aides and greater skilled nursing hours would dramatically improve quality of life.

03 - HEARING
Hearing Aids

Traditional Medicare does not cover hearing aids, despite hearing loss being one of the most prevalent conditions among seniors. Without coverage, costs of $3,000 to $7,000 per pair are entirely out-of-pocket. Untreated hearing loss is directly linked to cognitive decline, social isolation, and depression.

04 - SUPPLIES
Medical Supplies

Coverage for durable medical equipment and everyday medical supplies is inconsistent, bureaucratic, and often insufficient. Reimbursement rates fail to keep pace with actual costs, leaving beneficiaries to navigate complex prior authorization processes for items as basic as wound care supplies and diabetic monitoring equipment.

05 - DENTAL
Dental Coverage

Original Medicare provides virtually no dental coverage, excluding routine cleanings, fillings, extractions, and dentures. Poor oral health is directly correlated with cardiovascular disease, diabetes complications, and malnutrition. This is one of the most glaring structural oversights in the program.

06 - VISION
Vision Coverage

Routine vision care, including eye exams, eyeglasses, and contact lenses, is not covered under traditional Medicare. Vision deterioration accelerates with age and contributes significantly to fall risk, reduced mobility, and loss of independence, representing both a quality-of-life and safety gap.

07 - TRANSPARENCY
Price Transparency

Beneficiaries are routinely subjected to procedures and specialist visits without any prior disclosure of cost-sharing obligations. The inability to obtain an upfront, reliable estimate of out-of-pocket expenses prevents informed decision-making and contributes to medical debt and financial shock.

08 - SURGERY
Laser Cataract Surgery

Medicare covers traditional cataract surgery but does not cover laser-assisted techniques, which offer greater precision and reduced complications. Excluding it from coverage creates a two-tier system where only wealthier beneficiaries can access the safest and most effective surgical option.

09 - PRESCRIPTIONS
PDP Plan Availability and Drug Costs

The Part D landscape is marked by unpredictable premium increases, wide variation in formulary coverage, and rising cost-sharing tiers that can make essential medications unaffordable. Plan availability is often limited in rural and underserved areas, overwhelming many beneficiaries.

10 - PHYSICIANS
Physician Reimbursement Rates

Medicare reimbursement rates for physicians have chronically lagged behind the actual cost of delivering care. A growing number of providers are limiting or discontinuing Medicare patient acceptance, directly shrinking the network available to seniors.

11 - TRANSPORTATION
Non-Emergency Medical Transportation

Medicare's coverage of transportation to medical appointments is largely limited to emergency situations. For elderly individuals who no longer drive, coverage for ambulette and non-emergency medical transportation is not a luxury. It is a prerequisite for receiving care at all.

12 - SOCIAL SECURITY
Social Security Benefit Adequacy

The average Social Security retirement benefit falls significantly short of covering basic living expenses in most American cities. Monthly payments frequently do not account for the real cost of housing, property taxes, utilities, food, and prescription medications, particularly as inflation continues to outpace Cost-of-Living Adjustments.
